One logistical note before you go: parking near BOA is a real problem. Budget extra time, use a rideshare, or make peace with a walk. Once you're inside, that irritation evaporates.

The room runs warm and energetic without tipping into loud. The hospitality is the clearest differentiator — staff are genuinely attentive, know the menu cold, and handle the table like they actually care about the outcome of your night. That's rarer than it should be at a steakhouse at this price point, and BOA gets it right.

The cocktail list is the first signal that this isn't a paint-by-numbers steakhouse program. The menu is creative in a way that reads like someone actually experimented — unusual base spirits, layered builds, garnishes that earn their place. The drinks arrive looking the part: egg white foam, floral garnishes, well-chosen glassware. It's inventive without being precious, which is the hard balance to hit.

Food is the steakhouse canon executed cleanly. Parker House rolls land at the table first — pillowy, well-salted, served with two butters you'll blow through faster than intended. The tableside Caesar is a reliable anchor. The filet is the move: a well-portioned cut, cooked correctly, with a tight selection of sauces (the chimichurri holds its own). The sticky toffee pudding-style dessert closed the meal strong and came plated with a nice personal touch.

The menu also swings for the upscale end — caviar program, Japanese A5 Wagyu flight, Four Sixes Ranch ribeye — which puts BOA in a different tier than most Austin steak spots if you want to go all in.

**The verdict:** Worth it. Solve the parking problem in advance and enjoy the rest.
